James Harper, PE, ENV SP

Researcher, Engineer, Teacher

Hi!  I'm James Harper, Civil Engineering PhD student at the University of Colorado Boulder.  I research how human behavior affects fecal sludge management in rural developing communities in Southeast Asia.  I'm a registered Professional Mechanical Engineer and have taught STEM for over 10 years.  I'm from Gilroy, California but currently live in Broomfield, Colorado with my spouse and two dogs.

Research

Pit latrines are used daily by the 3.4 billion people living in rural areas around the world.  When pits fill, options are limited to pit replication or emptying, which are expensive and are rarely performed hygienically in favor of cheaper, less effective, and dangerous methods.  My research describes how human behavior affects fecal sludge management (FSM) in rural developing communities.

  • INTENTIONS WHEN PITS FILL

    The intentions of rural latrine owners towards fecal sludge management of their family's waste are poorly understood and critical to sustainable sanitation in developing communities.  This formative research describes the FSM intentions of rural latrine owners in Cambodia.

  • EFFECTS OF BEHAVIOR ON FSM

    While few studies have investigated how human behavior affects FSM in rural developing communities, a systematic literature review and meta-analysis will be performed to identify what is currently known and unknown about how behavior affects FSM.

  • PAST EFFORTS TO CHANGe FSM BEHAVIORS

    Behavior change techniques have been used to attempt to change FSM behaviors in prior studies.  A systematic literature review and meta-analysis will be performed to identify what behavior change techniques have been successful at changing FSM behaviors.

  • BEHAVIORS WHEN PITS FILL

    What rural latrine owners decide to do when their latrine pits fill is poorly understood but has strong effects on public health and the environment.  This formative research will describe what rural latrine owners in Cambodia do when their pits fill (i.e., their FSM behaviors).

  • MOTIVATORS AND BARRIERS TO FSM BEHAVIORS

    What motivates rural latrine owners to adopt certain FSM behaviors is unknown and must be understood to improve FSM in rural developing communities.  Qualitative methods will be used to identify the motivators and barriers to FSM behaviors, and quantitative methods will be used to describe variations in these behaviors in communities in 10 provinces of Cambodia.

  • BEHAVIOR CHANGE FRAMEWORKS FOR FSM

    Various behavior change frameworks have been developed for different disciplines.  Relevant frameworks will be evaluated for application to FSM in rural developing communities.

  • CHANGING FSM BEHAVIORS

    Having described FSM intentions and behaviors through qualitative and quantitative field studies, and systematic literature reviews, an FSM behavior change campaign will be performed in rural communities of Cambodia.  Effectiveness will be measured over short and long terms using follow-up qualitative surveying.
